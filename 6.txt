Nuragic Bronze Statuettes
These horned figurines bring to light a mysterious people whose origin is hotly debated

In the 1200s BCE, the Ancient Mediterranean was hit by a catastrophe. Several mysterious groups around the Mediterranean banded together to attack the Bronze Age's most important civilizations. Called the "Sea Peoples" by Ramses III, they took the Mediterranean area by storm. Ugarit, the Hittites, Cyprus, the Canaanites, and even the mighty Egyptians all faced attacks by these mysterious people.

This triggered a widespread event called the Bronze Age Collapse, where many powerful nations disappeared or became much less prosperous. Only Egypt retained its power.

Archaeologists have been able to identify the origin of some of these groups (notably the Peleset, who came from the Aegean), but many remain a mystery.

One group of the Sea Peoples that straddle this line is a group called the Sherden or Shardana (the original Egyptian is šrdn). In Egyptian art, these people are depicted as wearing horned helmets.

What makes this group so mysterious is their connection to the island of Sardinia. Already, you can see a connection in the name; this is attested by the Nora Stone, a Phoenician inscription that labels the island as šrdn, the same name as these Sea Peoples.

Similarly compelling are the figurines found on the island. Called the Nuragic Bronze Statuettes, they depict warriors wearing horned helmets that look suspiciously like those depicted by Ramses III as the Sherden.

The mystery is this: did the Sherden hail from Sardinia, or did they settle in Sardinia after their many exploits in the Eastern Mediterranean? Historians are still debating this question.
