Mithras Liturgy Papyrus
A papyrus describing a mystical soul journey might be a window into Mithraic theology

During the Roman period, few religious groups were as mysterious as the followers of the god Mithras. Worshipped primarily by soldiers, Mithras was a solar deity loosely inspired by a Zoroastrian god.

This religion was based on initiation rites, a hierarchy among devotees, ritual meals, and astrology. All of this was expected to be kept secret by the initiates, meaning that historians have few religious texts. Mithraic theology is not well known as a result.

One surviving text can shed light on this mysterious religion, but it raises more questions than it provides answers. This text is the Mithras Liturgy Papyrus. Dating anywhere from the 2nd to the 4th centuries CE, this text describes a mystical experience in which the soul journeys through seven stages to reach divine revelation.Historians have debated what all this means for decades. Some believe that this text reveals Mithraism to be a religion based around the immortality of the soul (like Christianity), but others believe that this text doesn't actually describe commonly held beliefs about Mithras. Other historians don't even believe it has anything to do with Mithraism at all!

Everything about this text is shrouded in mystery, from its origins to its interpretation.
